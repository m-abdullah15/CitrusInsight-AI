January
  • In orchards where the fruits have been picked, complete the pruning process - leave the dead, diseased branches and dead leaves on the trees.
  • Keep an eye on baby mealybugs by spraying control where they appear. For this purpose, apply chloropyriphos 25 ml of methidathion 25 ml or profenofos 25 ml per liter of water on tree trunks from two to two and a half feet above the ground and in the canopy of the plant. Spray on the ground below
  • Take measures to protect plants from the harmful effects of fog, especially by pruning and thinning trees like bamboo or other tall trees around the orchards, to allow sunlight and air to pass through the orchards.
  • Anticipating frosty nights, make sure to apply a light watering to the orchards.
  • If organic fertilizer was not applied in December, ensure to apply 50 to 60 kilograms of well-rotted manure in January. Avoid relying solely on poultry manure.

  • After applying fertilizers, irrigate the orchards.
  • Carry out the task of inspecting the ripeness and harvesting of fruit during the day after the dew has dried.

Citrus Mealybug

February
  • Continue pruning for better fruit production. This is essential for controlling citrus scab, melanose, canker, and other diseases. Burn the pruned branches and twigs.
  • Apply NPK or Nitrophos, DAP + Sulfate of Potash in the second week of February. If using NPK, there is no need for additional Sulfate of Potash.
  • Apply the remaining nitrogenous fertilizer in three doses (end of February, end of April, and the third dose in August-September). Additionally, apply three bags of Nitrophos or NPK per acre for ten-year-old or older plants in the second week of February. In this case, there is no need to apply additional nitrogenous fertilizer in February. However, for the remaining fertilizer, apply 2 bags of Nitrophos, NPK, or Ammonium Nitrate per acre in August-September.
  • If fungal sprays were not applied in January, immediately spray one of the following fungicides after pruning to control diseases: Thiophanate Methyl (2 grams per liter of water), Difenoconazole (1 milliliter per liter of water), or Bordeaux Mixture (1:1:100 ratio).
  • Use a rotavator in the orchards to remove weeds, ensuring plants are not damaged.
  • Complete fertilizer application and irrigation by February to create favorable conditions for flowering and to prevent excessive vegetative growth.
  • Complete fruit harvesting to allow the plants to prepare for the next crop season.
  • Spray appropriate pesticides to control aphids and mealybugs.
March
  • Adopt the method of irrigation through trenches in orchards to conserve water. The size of the trenches should be 12 feet in width and depth.
  • By the end of March, spray the following pesticides in combination to control pests such as thrips, leaf miners, aphids, lemon butterfly, etc., and diseases like citrus scab, citrus melanose, and citrus canker.
  • Ensure pruning is completed in the orchards before spraying so that the spray can be effective.
  • Start grafting on nursery plants, such as rootstock.
  • Begin preparing the land for planting new orchards, ensuring no weeds, bushes, or remnants of trees such as roots remain in the soil.
  • Bury fallen diseased fruits in a 3-foot-deep pit to protect the plants from germs.

  • In case of zinc deficiency, apply 50 grams of Zengro per plant by the end of April.
  • Apply Bordeaux paste to tree trunks to prevent bark splitting during summer. Mix 1 kg blue vitriol, 2 kg slaked lime, and 12 liters of water (12:2:1 ratio) and apply it up to 2 to 2.5 feet from the ground level on the trunk.

Citrus Scab

May
  • Irrigate large citrus orchards every 15 days, while small plants should be watered every 7 to 10 days.
  • Apply mulching around the trees up to their canopy spread. Keep the mulch 2 feet away from the main trunk and use materials such as grass clippings, green straw, palm or bamboo leaves with a thickness of up to 15 cm for mulching.
  • Decomposed leaves, tree branches, and crop residues can also be used as compost for mulching.
  • Continuously remove sprouts below the grafted part on the rootstock of nursery plants.
June
  • For trees where mealybugs have reached the ground in their adult stage, perform light hoeing to destroy their eggs.
  • Wherever the mulch layer has reduced, replenish and level it properly.
  • To prevent fruit drop, ensure proper irrigation. Water every 10 days or as per the tensiometer scale. If the tensiometer scale exceeds 40-50, irrigate immediately.
July
  • If there is no rain, irrigate the orchards at an interval of 10 to 15 days.
  • To control fruit flies, install 6 to 8 pheromone traps per acre.
  • Take measures to install windbreak barriers around the orchards.
  • Preferably, plant neem trees around the orchards during the monsoon season.
  • Monitor fungal diseases and, as soon as rains begin and a break occurs, spray Copper Oxychloride or Copper Hydroxide at a rate of 3 grams per liter of water, or Bordeaux mixture, for the control of citrus scab, citrus melanose, citrus canker, and stem-end rot (citrus wither tip).
  • During the rainy season, pests like citrus psylla, citrus scales, and cottony cushion scales become active. To control them, use any of the following insecticides: Bard at 2.5 ml per liter of water, or Nova Star at 1.25 ml per liter of water, ensuring to mix one tablespoon of detergent in each spray machine.
  • For cottony cushion scales, spraying neem oil at a rate of 10 ml per liter of water can yield effective results.
August
  • Monitor citrus orchards and identify declining trees. After diagnosing the causes of decline, work towards restoring their health. Preferably, after pruning the diseased branches, apply the following mixture: Rugby 70 grams + Copper Sulfate 250 grams + Ridomil Gold 80 grams + Humic Acid 2 kg per large tree, followed by irrigation.
  • Select land for the nursery that has good drainage, salinity below 1000 ppm, is not affected by waterlogging or salinity, and has a pH level between 7 and 8.
  • Harvest healthy fruit from rootstocks and place it in a clean area to extract seeds.
  • To improve the productivity of sweet lime, apply 1.5 kg of urea per tree at the end of August.
  • Use the following insecticides to control pests and diseases.
  • For the prevention of citrus scab, citrus melanose, and citrus canker, spray Bordeaux mixture (1%) or Champion (Copper Hydroxide) at the rate of 3 grams per liter of water.
  • To control psylla and other sap-sucking insects, spray Imidacloprid at 1.25 ml or Acetamiprid at 1.25 ml per liter of water. Ensure that no other pesticide is mixed with the fungicide Champion.
  • After harvesting sweet lime fruit, perform proper pruning to remove vertical shoots, crowded branches, and branches forming narrow angles.
  • Complete fruit harvesting for sweet lime according to standard methods.
  • Start preparing the land for planting new orchards.
  • Apply the third installment of nitrogen fertilizer by using 700 grams of Engro Urea or 1200 grams of Ammonium Nitrate, followed by irrigation.
  • Create ponds to drain excess rainwater from the orchard and store it in suitable locations.
  • Considering weather conditions, irrigate the orchards preferably using furrow irrigation methods.

Citrus Canker

September
  • To control various pests such as citrus leaf miner, psylla, and other sap-sucking insects, spray Acetamiprid at 1.25 ml or Novastar at 1.25 ml per liter of water.
  • Prepare the land for planting new orchards. After marking the layout, dig pits and start the plantation process.
  • For fruit dropping, citrus scab, or citrus melanose control, spray Copper U-Top at 2 grams per liter of water, Nextro Pro at 1 ml per liter of water, or Tops M at 2 grams per liter of water.
  • Apply the remaining amount of NPK fertilizer, ranging from half a kilogram to one kilogram depending on the tree's age, in September.
  • In case of a deficiency of micronutrients, prepare a solution of the respective elements and spray accordingly.
October
  • To control citrus scab and melanose, spray Nativo at 65 grams per 200 liters of water or Rally at 40 grams per 200 liters of water. Use appropriate methods to control fruit fly (male and female).
  • Ensure to fill gaps in the orchards.
  • Apply paste on the tree trunks up to 2 to 2.5 feet above ground level according to the proportions mentioned in the calendar.
  • Make suitable arrangements for the sale of early varieties of oranges to ensure they fetch a good price.
  • Extract seeds from the fruit of the rootstock and keep them in a shaded area for a few days. Then treat them with an appropriate fungicide, preferably Ridomil Gold or Topsin M, at the rate of 2 grams per kilogram of seeds before planting.
  • After the above procedure, sow the seeds on raised beds with dimensions of 6 feet in length, 4 feet in width, and 15 cm in height from the ground. Sow the seeds in 10 mm deep furrows, maintaining a distance of 10 cm between rows.

Fruit fly

November
  • Inspect all agricultural equipment and ensure they are in working condition.
  • Begin harvesting various varieties of oranges and, after pruning them post-harvest, spray with any fungicide, preferably Elite at 1 gram per liter or Copper Oxychloride at 3 grams per liter of water.
  • Dispose of all fallen fruit, especially those affected by disease or pests.
  • Perform light tilling around the plants to destroy mealybug eggs.
  • Arrange for organic fertilizers so that they can be applied in a well-decomposed state in December.
  • Start harvesting different varieties of oranges after assessing their ripeness.

Citrus Silla(Tella)

December
  • Focus special attention on protecting plants from the harmful effects of cold and apply light irrigation to orchards on frosty nights.
  • To eliminate insects emerging from mealybug eggs, spray appropriate insecticides, and limit the spray to areas where mealybug larvae are visible. Preferably, spray Chlorpyrifos or Profenofos at 3 ml per liter of water up to 3 feet height on the tree trunk to effectively control mealybug larvae.
  • Ensure the application of organic fertilizers to plants in December.
  • Start harvesting Kinnow oranges at the end of December, as by then, the fruit reaches its acceptable level of ripeness and internal sweetness. However, the harvesting process should be carried out using standard methods after assessing ripeness.
